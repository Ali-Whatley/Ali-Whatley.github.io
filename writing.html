An overview of my writing on international relations & defense

Originally published on Substack

I write about international security, political economy, and the changing nature of conflict. My thinking is shaped by a core observation: the post-Cold War era hasn't brought the peace liberal theorists predicted—it's transformed how states fight, not whether they fight.

Economic interdependence doesn't prevent conflict. It reshapes it. States with deep trade ties don't stop competing—they compete differently. This explains why gray-zone tactics, cyber operations, and economic coercion have proliferated alongside globalization, not despite it.

Core themes

Trade and conflict intensity. I argue that trade vulnerability—the interaction of bilateral dependence and limited alternatives—constrains escalation rather than preventing conflict entirely. High vulnerability pushes states toward lower-intensity tactics; low vulnerability permits conventional warfare. This reframes the liberal peace debate from conflict occurrence to conflict conduct.

Gray-zone competition. The proliferation of gray-zone tactics isn't a bug of the international system—it's a feature. States facing economic constraints on escalation don't stop pursuing their objectives; they pursue them through cyber operations, economic statecraft, and sub-threshold coercion. Understanding this requires moving beyond binary war/peace frameworks.

Cyber and emerging domains. The 2017 WannaCry and NotPetya attacks demonstrated how quickly offensive capabilities can proliferate—an American exploit, leaked by hackers, weaponized by North Korea and Russia within months. This pattern of capability diffusion shapes how I think about technological competition and arms control in new domains.

International institutions. I'm interested in how international organizations create standards that enable cooperation without requiring states to trust each other. Maritime safety regimes like IMSO show how technical standardization can produce collective benefits even among competitors.

What I'm working on

My current research examines how trade structure affects conflict intensity using a mixed-methods design combining large-N analysis of directed dyads with comparative case studies of Russia-Ukraine, China-Taiwan, and Ethiopia-Eritrea. I'm also tracking how decoupling trends post-2014 are reshaping these dynamics.

More broadly, I'm interested in energy security, economic statecraft, technological proliferation, and the political economy of defense—all variations on the same question: how do economic relationships shape strategic behavior?
